Preventing sinking of a disk by leveraging the boundary jump phenomenon

Authors: Jan Turczynowicz, Radost Waszkiewicz, Łukasz Gładczuk

In this work we show that a metal disk, which would normally just sink in water, can actually stay afloat if a thin water jet falls onto it. The jet creates a structure simmilar to hydraulic jump that pushes water away and gives the disk an additional upward force. We worked out when this effect appears and confirmed it experimentally.

Bridging advection and diffusion in the encounter dynamics of sedimenting marine snow

Authors: Jan Turczynowicz, Radost Waszkiewicz, Jonasz Słomka, Maciej Lisicki

Marine snow, the tiny organic particles that sink through the ocean, constantly meets and collects microorganisms on the way down. We looked at how often these encounters really happen. Although you'd expect advection to dominate completely for a sinking particle, diffusion still plays a surprisingly important role. We derived a formula that connects both effects and showed that ignoring diffusion can hugely underestimate how often marine snow traps small particles. We have showed that the encounters are governed by two dimensionless numbers instead of just the Peclet number.

Under review in Journal of Fluid Mechanics

Drzewo, jakie jest, każdy widzi

Authors: Jan Turczynowicz, Radost Waszkiewicz

Why trees grow higher than 10 m? The answer of this question provides an interesting insight into a metastable state of water inside a trunk of a high tree. Also we show how it relates to a very first methode of measuring the atmospheric pressure.

Have you ever seen a tree that is higher then 10 m?

Krótka wycieczka po wielkościach bezwymiarowych

Authors: Jan Turczynowicz

A light introduction to why physicists love dimensionless numbers. I show how rewriting a problem without units often makes the physics clearer, helps you guess how things scale, and generally makes life easier when solving physical problems.

If you are interested in usage of dimensionless numbers.

Nawijanie miodu

Authors: Jan Turczynowicz, Radost Waszkiewicz

A short piece on why honey behaves the way it does when it flows, stretches, or “winds up.” It’s an excuse to talk about viscosity, scaling, and fluid-mechanics intuition — using something everyone has in their kitchen.
